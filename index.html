Food energy is the energy that humans and animals derive from their food, through the process of cellular respiration. Cell respiration involves the chemical reaction of food molecules (like glucose) with molecular oxygen to produce carbon dioxide, water, and energy (ATP).

Common units used to measure food energy are:

  • Calorie (Nutritional, kcal): The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kilogram of water by 1°C. Equivalent to 1,000 small calories or 1 kilocalorie. This is the unit commonly found on food nutrition labels.
  • calorie (cal): The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1°C. 1 kcal = 1,000 cal.
  • Kilojoules (kJ): The metric unit of food energy. 1 Calorie (kcal) is equivalent to approximately 4.184 Kilojoules (kJ).
  • Joules (J): The SI unit of energy. 1 kJ = 1,000 J.

Formula used for conversion: 1 kcal = 4.184 kJ = 4184 J = 1000 cal.

Our calorie calculator estimates the daily calorie requirements needed to maintain, lose, or gain weight based on several scientifically validated formulas for Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR):

Mifflin-St Jeor Equation

Considered the most accurate standard formula for estimating BMR in healthy individuals without measured body fat percentage:

Men:   BMR = 10 * weight(kg) + 6.25 * height(cm) - 5 * age(years) + 5
Women: BMR = 10 * weight(kg) + 6.25 * height(cm) - 5 * age(years) - 161

Revised Harris-Benedict Equation

An older but highly popular formula revised in 1984 by Roza and Shizgal:

Men:   BMR = 13.397 * weight(kg) + 4.799 * height(cm) - 5.677 * age(years) + 88.362
Women: BMR = 9.247 * weight(kg) + 3.098 * height(cm) - 4.330 * age(years) + 447.593

Katch-McArdle Formula

Estimates BMR based on lean body mass (LBM). Ideal for individuals who know their body fat percentage:

BMR = 370 + 21.6 * LBM
Where LBM = weight(kg) * (1 - body_fat_percentage / 100)

Once BMR is calculated, it is multiplied by your activity level factor (ranging from 1.0 for BMR only, to 1.9 for extra active) to estimate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE). Caloric deficits or surpluses are then applied to establish your weight change goals.
